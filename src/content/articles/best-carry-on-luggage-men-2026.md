Most carry-on buying guides are written by people who've never missed a flight because their bag didn't fit in the overhead bin. This one isn't.

The standards here: fits in a standard overhead bin (22" x 14" x 9" or smaller), survives actual travel, has a warranty that means something, and doesn't look like it came from a duty-free sale. All tested against real airline restrictions. All worth buying.

---

## What Matters in a Carry-On

**Dimensions**: The safe window is 22" x 14" x 9" (56 x 36 x 23 cm). Some airlines are stricter. Measure including wheels and handles. Several "carry-on" cases sold online fail this test. Verify before you buy.

**Hard-side vs. soft-side**: Hard-side protects better, more water resistant, doesn't absorb spills. Soft-side has external pockets (useful), compresses slightly to squeeze into tight overhead bins. Both have their place. Hard-side is generally the better call for a primary bag.

**Spinner vs. two-wheel**: Four-wheel spinners are easier to maneuver in terminals. Two-wheel rollers are more durable on rough surfaces. Most people should buy the spinner.

**TSA-approved locks**: Not optional if you check bags. For carry-ons, matters less but still useful for peace of mind.

**Warranty**: Lifetime warranty is the benchmark. Rimowa and Away offer it. Some brands don't — that's information.

## Airlines to Watch

**Basic economy on budget carriers**: Spirit, Frontier, and Ryanair have smaller personal item limits and charge for overhead bin bags. Check dimensions before you fly, not after you pack.

**International airlines**: Many allow 21" x 15" x 9" or similar. The Away and Rimowa cases technically exceed standard US dimensions — they get away with it most of the time, but you're taking a small risk.

**Weight**: Most US carriers don't weigh carry-ons. International carriers sometimes do (especially in business class). A lighter bag gives you more packing room.
